# Running.Services Directory Rebuild Report

## Executive Summary

Running.Services should be rebuilt as a race decision engine, not a generic race calendar. The current category opportunity is real: the U.S. endurance race market is large, fragmented, mobile-driven, and still growing, but generic race listings are already commoditized by RunSignup, RunGuides, Find a Race, RaceRaves, Ahotu, Runner’s World, timing companies, and local clubs.

The winning thesis is simple:

> Runners do not need another list of races. They need help choosing the right race.

The product should become the “Zillow for running races”: a structured, mobile-first directory that compares 5Ks, 10Ks, half marathons, marathons, trail races, Turkey Trots, charity races, and fun runs by price, distance, date, terrain, difficulty, vibe, registration urgency, runner-friendliness, weather, and logistics.

The differentiated product should answer:

- Which race should I do?
- Is it beginner-friendly?
- Is it flat and fast?
- Is it worth the price?
- Can I bring my kid, stroller, dog, or walking group?
- Is registration about to get more expensive?
- What are similar alternatives nearby?
- Is this race trusted, recurring, and well-organized?

The directory should not try to compete with RunSignup as a registration platform. RunSignup processed $650 million for 39,000 events in 2025, says it has more than 50% U.S. endurance market share, and powers registration, websites, email, fundraising, and race-day tools ([RunSignup](https://info.runsignup.com/2026/01/02/2025-year-in-review/)). Running.Services should instead become the independent discovery, comparison, and decision layer above registration platforms.

The rebuild should focus on four product layers:

| Layer | Purpose | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Race database | Normalize every race into comparable metadata. | A directory is only as valuable as its data quality. |
| Decision engine | Score races by fit, difficulty, price, vibe, and urgency. | This is the core differentiation versus simple listings. |
| Alerts and saved searches | Retain users around race intent. | Directories need memory and timing to become repeat products. |
| Organizer claim tools | Create supply-side engagement and monetization. | Race directors need visibility, corrections, coupons, and analytics. |

The first wedge should be local, seasonal, and decision-rich: Turkey Trots, beginner-friendly 5Ks, half marathons, flat/fast marathons, and city-specific race pages.

## Market Context

The race market is healthy enough to support a discovery product. RunSignup’s 2025 RaceTrends report says race participation grew 5% from 2024 to 2025, after 8% growth in 2024 and 11% growth in 2023 ([RunSignup](https://info.runsignup.com/2026/02/02/2025-race-trends-report/)). RunSignup’s dataset covers more than 97,000 race events and 12.2 million registrations, and the company estimates that it represents at least 50% of the U.S. endurance market ([RunSignup](https://info.runsignup.com/2026/02/02/2025-race-trends-report/)).

The market is fragmented in a way that favors directories. RunSignup reports that 87% of races have fewer than 500 participants, while those small races account for 38% of participants ([RunSignup](https://info.runsignup.com/2026/02/02/2025-race-trends-report/)). Running USA’s coverage of the same report says 86% of race weekends host fewer than 500 participants, less than 1% of events exceed 5,000 participants, and only 13% of participants are in large races over 5,000 people ([Running USA](https://www.runningusa.org/industry-news/runsignups-annual-racetrends-report-shows-per-race-participation-growth-of-5/)).

The implication is important: most races are small, local, and discovery-constrained. Big races already have brand awareness, lotteries, sponsors, press coverage, and returning audiences. Small races need discovery, credibility, comparison, and better distribution.

Mobile behavior is decisive. RunSignup says 74% of race website views and 63% of transactions came from mobile or tablet devices, and 42% of participants paid with Apple Pay or a saved credit card in November ([RunSignup](https://info.runsignup.com/2026/02/02/2025-race-trends-report/)). Running.Services should therefore be built mobile-first: location search, distance chips, map/list toggle, save, compare, alert, and register.

Prices are rising, creating a strong opportunity for price intelligence. RunSignup says prices rose across all distances for the third year in a row, and 10K prices are up 21% since 2019 ([RunSignup](https://info.runsignup.com/2026/02/02/2025-race-trends-report/)). A directory that tracks price tiers, registration deadlines, price increases, and “best value” races can become more useful than a static calendar.

Seasonality creates a strong wedge. Running USA reports that November, the Turkey Trot month, had the highest race growth rate at 10% ([Running USA](https://www.runningusa.org/industry-news/runsignups-annual-racetrends-report-shows-per-race-participation-growth-of-5/)). Running Insight’s coverage says 10.1% of all events occurred in November, and a quarter of November races took place on a single day ([Running Insight](https://www.runninginsight.com/runsignup-report-finds-the-good-and-bad-of-2025)). Running.Services should build the best Turkey Trot finder on the internet before trying to own every running query.

## Competitive Landscape

### RunSignup

RunSignup is the dominant U.S. endurance registration infrastructure platform. It processed $650 million for 39,000 events in 2025, up from $562 million in 2024, and claims more than 50% market share in U.S. endurance events ([RunSignup](https://info.runsignup.com/2026/01/02/2025-year-in-review/)).

RunSignup’s strengths are infrastructure, organizer tools, registration, fundraising, email, websites, race-day check-in, and payments. Its weakness as a competitor to Running.Services is that it is not neutral discovery. It is a platform where registration infrastructure and event management are central.

Strategic implication: do not compete with RunSignup on registration. Use RunSignup and other registration platforms as outbound destinations, and compete on independent search, comparison, alerts, and decision support.

### RunGuides

RunGuides is a direct directory competitor. Its North America page lists races by date, size, and location, includes featured placements, and shows coupon codes such as “Get 10% Off” for selected events ([RunGuides](https://www.runguides.com/north-america/runs)).

RunGuides’ strengths are clean listings, event size metadata, featured placements, organizer CTAs, and coupon monetization. Its weakness is that the experience remains mostly a list. It does not deeply answer “which race is right for me?” or expose rich decision fields such as difficulty, value, family-friendliness, course profile, price history, or registration urgency.

Strategic implication: Running.Services should not out-list RunGuides. It should out-decide RunGuides.

### Find a Race

Find a Race has strong SEO framing. Its U.S. running page targets “Running Near Me | US Events Calendar 2026 - 2027” and promises a comprehensive list of U.S. running events with filters by location, month, distance, and terrain ([Find a Race](https://findarace.com/us/running)).

Find a Race’s strengths are SEO page construction, broad coverage, and user-friendly category framing. Its weakness is that it is broad and still primarily calendar-driven.

Strategic implication: Running.Services should borrow the structured SEO model but add richer race-fit metadata and decision tools.

### RaceRaves

RaceRaves’ strongest concept is reviews. Its San Francisco race page targets “2026-2027 Races in San Francisco, California” and emphasizes marathons, half marathons, 10Ks, 5Ks, and honest finisher reviews ([RaceRaves](https://raceraves.com/california-races-reviews/san-francisco/)).

RaceRaves’ strength is community proof. Its weakness is that review density is hard to scale, and the product is not obviously a modern decision engine.

Strategic implication: Running.Services should incorporate lightweight reviews, but should not depend entirely on user-generated reviews for value. It should use structured race intelligence even when reviews are sparse.

## Strategic Positioning

The current positioning should be replaced.

Current-style positioning:

> The comprehensive USA race calendar and route directory.

Recommended positioning:

> Find the right race, not just the next race.

Expanded positioning:

> Running.Services helps runners compare races by price, difficulty, distance, terrain, vibe, logistics, and registration urgency so they can choose the best 5K, 10K, half marathon, marathon, trail race, or Turkey Trot near them.

Category:

> Race discovery and decision engine.

Not:

- Race registration software
- Running analytics
- Strava coaching
- Generic running content hub
- Route directory
- Blog
- Event-management SaaS

For runners, Running.Services should be the independent race shopping layer.

For organizers, Running.Services should be the free race listing, claim, correction, and promotion layer.

For sponsors and brands, Running.Services should be a local running-intent audience.

## Product Thesis

The core product thesis:

> A race directory becomes valuable when it helps runners make decisions, not when it merely lists events.

Most race directories treat all races as rows in a calendar. Running.Services should treat races as products that can be compared.

The race page should resemble a hybrid of:

- Zillow listing
- Google Flights comparison
- Yelp review page
- Wirecutter-style recommendation
- Kayak alert system

The product should make race discovery feel like shopping for the right event.

## Core User Segments

### Beginner runners

Beginner runners need confidence. They ask whether a race is manageable, welcoming, walker-friendly, family-friendly, affordable, and easy to attend.

Build for:

- “first 5K”
- “beginner-friendly half marathon”
- “walker-friendly 5K”
- “family fun run”
- “stroller-friendly race”
- “cheap 5K near me”

### Goal-oriented runners

Goal-oriented runners care about PRs, course profile, field size, weather, competition, travel, and timing.

Build for:

- “flat fast half marathon”
- “Boston qualifier marathon”
- “PR-friendly 10K”
- “spring marathon tune-up race”
- “fall half marathon near me”

### Social runners

Social runners care about friends, run clubs, themes, beer, costumes, music, charity, and post-race events.

Build for:

- “beer run”
- “Turkey Trot”
- “Halloween 5K”
- “Jingle Bell run”
- “charity 5K”
- “run club races”

### Parents and families

Families care about kids races, parking, bathrooms, strollers, start time, safety, and post-race activities.

Build for:

- “kids fun run”
- “family 5K”
- “stroller-friendly 5K”
- “Thanksgiving family run”

### Race organizers

Race organizers need participants, visibility, accurate listings, discounts, timing partner visibility, and local marketing.

Build for:

- claim page
- correct listing
- add price/deadline/coupon
- promote locally
- view outbound clicks
- benchmark against similar races

## Keyword Strategy

Running.Services should not start by trying to win broad national terms like “running races,” “race calendar,” or “marathon calendar USA.” Those terms are competitive and often already served by large incumbents.

The SEO strategy should start with structured local, seasonal, and decision-intent pages.

### Keyword Matrix

| Intent class | Example queries | Value |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Local discovery | “5K near me,” “running races near me,” “half marathons near NYC” | High-volume discovery |
| Distance + location | “10K races in Chicago,” “marathons in California,” “trail half marathon Colorado” | Best programmatic SEO foundation |
| Date + distance | “May 5K races near me,” “April half marathons,” “fall marathons 2026” | Strong planning intent |
| Seasonal | “Turkey Trot near me,” “Thanksgiving 5K NYC,” “Halloween 5K Chicago” | Excellent event-specific intent |
| Qualitative | “best beginner half marathons,” “flat fast marathons,” “scenic 10K races” | Differentiated content |
| Constraint-based | “walker-friendly 5K,” “stroller-friendly race,” “dog-friendly 5K” | Underserved by incumbents |
| Price-sensitive | “cheap 5K near me,” “free fun run,” “race discount codes” | Monetizable and practical |
| Social/vibe | “beer run,” “charity 5K,” “run club races” | Matches social running trends |

### Page Architecture

| Page type | URL pattern | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| City + distance | `/city-state/5k-races` | `/new-york-ny/5k-races` |
| City + distance + month | `/city-state/5k-races/may` | `/chicago-il/10k-races/october` |
| State + distance | `/state/half-marathons` | `/california/half-marathons` |
| Seasonal local | `/turkey-trots/city-state` | `/turkey-trots/new-york-ny` |
| Qualitative national | `/best/beginner-half-marathons` | `/best/flat-fast-marathons` |
| Constraint local | `/walker-friendly-5k/city-state` | `/stroller-friendly-5k/austin-tx` |
| Race detail | `/races/[race-slug]` | `/races/nyc-marathon` |
| Organizer | `/organizers/[organizer-slug]` | `/organizers/new-york-road-runners` |
| Series | `/series/[series-slug]` | `/series/rock-n-roll-running-series` |

### SEO Principle

Every indexable page must answer a decision question.

Bad page:

> List of races in Chicago.

Good page:

> Best 5K races in Chicago by date, price, beginner-friendliness, course type, and registration urgency.

Bad page:

> Race detail page with name, date, city, and registration link.

Good page:

> Race detail page with who it is best for, price, deadline, distance, terrain, course difficulty, historical weather, logistics, policies, alternatives, and organizer trust signals.

### Data Quality Rules

Every indexed race page must have:

- Name
- Date
- City/state
- At least one distance
- Registration or official website link
- Source URL
- Last verified date

Noindex pages that do not meet the minimum.

Every city/distance page must have:

- At least 5 active or upcoming races, or
- A strong editorial explanation plus nearby alternatives.

Noindex empty pages.

- Saved races
- Compare actions
- Alert signups
- Outbound registration clicks
- Return visits from alerts
- Claimed races
- Featured listing purchases

### Metrics Dashboard

| Metric | What it tells you |
| --- | --- |
| Search-to-save rate | Whether results are useful. |
| Search-to-register-click rate | Whether the directory influences action. |
| Race-page-to-outbound-click rate | Whether detail pages convert. |
| Alert signup rate | Whether users trust Running.Services to remember intent. |
| Alert open/click rate | Whether retention works. |
| Claimed race rate | Whether organizers see value. |
| Claimed-to-paid rate | Whether supply-side monetization works. |
| Organic landing-to-action rate | Whether SEO traffic is valuable. |
| Repeat user rate | Whether the directory has utility beyond one-off search. |

Do not use page views as the primary PMF metric. Page views can produce false confidence in a directory. A valuable race directory changes decisions.

## Final Recommendation

Rebuild Running.Services into a race marketplace-intelligence product.

The simplest product promise:

> Find the right race by price, distance, date, difficulty, vibe, and location.

The first wedge:

> Turkey Trots, beginner-friendly 5Ks, and half marathons in major U.S. metros.

The first product loop:

> Search → compare → save → alert → registration click.

The first organizer loop:

> Listed race → claim page → enrich listing → see clicks → pay for promotion.

The first business model:

> Featured listings, sponsored local/category pages, discount-code placement, race-page upgrades, and newsletter sponsorship.

The moat:

> Structured race metadata and decision scores that make races comparable in a way generic calendars do not.

The success test:

> If runners save races, set alerts, compare options, and click registration links, Running.Services has product value. If organizers claim pages and pay for visibility, it has marketplace value. If neither happens, it is only an SEO site.
